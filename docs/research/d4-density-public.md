## Zone Profiles

### 1. Brickell (ZIP: 33130, 33131)

Miami's Financial District commands the highest professional density per square mile in Miami-Dade. Over 60 international banks operate here — the largest concentration outside New York City [T2, Beacon Council]. 830 Brickell (650,000 SF, delivered 2023–2024) is 95%+ occupied with tenants including Citadel, Kirkland & Ellis, Sidley Austin, Rothschild & Co., Marsh Insurance, Thoma Bravo, Microsoft, and Banco Santander — over 90% of whom were new to the Miami market [T2]. Submarket vacancy sits at 3.7%, lowest in South Florida, with Class A rents hitting record highs of $200/SF at 830 Brickell [T2, WorldPropertyJournal 2024]. As of early 2026, Brickell ranks #1 in law-firm office demand, ahead of Downtown Miami and Coral Gables [T3, Law.com/Daily Business Review, Feb 2026]. The Beacon Council reports the county financial sector employs 150,000+ at $110,000+ average wages and contributes $27.7B annually to GRP [T2]. Brickell concentrates the premium tier of this cohort.

**Notable concentration:** Global private equity, hedge funds, bulge-bracket banking, Top 100 law firms, Big-4 advisory arms.

---

### 2. Coral Gables (ZIP: 33134, 33146)

Coral Gables is Miami-Dade's second-most-established professional office cluster, absorbing overflow from Brickell at ~$54/SF vs. Brickell's $108/SF [T2, CBRE Q2 2024]. The city's 49,802 residents earn a median household income of $134,216 [T1, ACS 2024], and the local employed population of 23,904 skews heavily professional: the top three employment sectors are Professional, Scientific & Technical Services (4,114 workers), Educational Services (3,056), and Health Care & Social Assistance (2,800) [T1, DataUSA]. Management (5,153) and Business & Financial Operations (2,757) are the top occupational categories [T1]. Office vacancy averaged 21.9% across all classes in 2024, but Class A product is tight, with City National Bank of Florida signing a 145,000 SF relocation here in 2024 [T2]. Industry tenants span banking, investment management, healthcare multinationals (including University of Miami with 20,000+ faculty/staff), Bacardi, Mercedes-Benz, and a dense attorney-firm corridor along Ponce de Leon Blvd [T2/T3].

**Notable concentration:** Multinational HQs, wealth management, university-adjacent professional services, real estate law.

---

### 3. Doral (ZIP: 33122, 33126, 33178)

Doral is the largest non-Brickell commercial node in Miami-Dade and holds the county's second-largest office submarket [T2]. By the city's own figures, it hosts over 12,000 businesses (Census-reported), 150,000 daily workers, 250 corporate HQs, 100 multinationals, and 16 Fortune 500 companies including Carnival Cruise Line, Univision, Telemundo, Blue Cross Blue Shield, and Perry Ellis International [T2, Doral Chamber of Commerce]. Approximately 3,000 logistics companies operate here, cementing it as the #1 logistics city in Miami-Dade; over 50 financial institutions also maintain presence [T2]. Industrial vacancy below 3% and 8–12% annual rent growth reflect sustained nearshoring demand [T2]. The professional services cohort is substantial but skews toward operations/supply-chain executives, Latin-market GMs, and import-export specialists rather than attorneys or CPAs in independent practice — making chapter messaging somewhat more specialized.

**Notable concentration:** Import/export leadership, Latin American business HQs, logistics/ops executives, corporate finance.

---

### 4. Kendall (ZIP: 33176, 33186, 33183)

Kendall is a large suburban employment basin — the CDP population exceeds 120,000 — with strong white-collar representation. Zip 33176 reports median HHI of $86,354 and 18.9% of households earning $200,000+ [T1, ACS]; 33186 median HHI is $93,462 [T1]. The most common occupations across Kendall are Management (5,230 people) and Office & Administrative Support (5,295 people) [T1, DataUSA]. Kendall Town Center area shows 85.7% of workers in professional or administrative roles [T1]. Physical office assets include Kendall Summit Office Park (173,000 SF) and Dadeland West Executive Park, with a tenant mix of attorneys, jewelers, insurers, and healthcare providers [T2, LoopNet/COFE Properties]. Presence of Baptist Hospital, numerous medical-practice clusters, and the Dadeland retail corridor generates a stable middle-market professional population. Major law and CPA firms are scattered across strip-mall offices rather than Class A towers, indicating independent practitioners over BigLaw/Big-4.

**Notable concentration:** Independent attorneys, CPAs, insurance agents, healthcare entrepreneurs, physician-practice owners.

### 5. Westchester / FIU Corridor (ZIP: 33155, 33165, 33174)

This zone spans the contiguous neighborhoods around Florida International University's main campus (344 acres, University Park, unincorporated Miami-Dade). Zip 33155 (Westchester/Coral Terrace) carries a median HHI of $81,158 and average HHI of $113,689, with 11.9% of households earning $200,000+ [T1, ACS]. The FIU corridor draws faculty, researchers, and university-affiliated entrepreneurs through StartUP FIU's incubator network, which operates three incubators specifically intended to extend entrepreneurial activity west of the Palmetto and north of the county — zones historically underserved by Brickell-centric networking [T2, StartUP FIU/Florida Trend]. Sweetwater (ZIP 33174, population ~19,500, median HHI $55,154) has 96.2% Hispanic population and a rising retail corridor catalyzed by FIU's 1,000-student housing expansion with 5,000 SF of integrated retail [T1/T2]. Professional density here is growing but still emerging — most professionals commute to Brickell or Coral Gables. A chapter here would function as a "founder/early-stage" cluster rather than a senior executive one.

**Notable concentration:** FIU-adjacent tech/startup founders, first-generation Latin-American entrepreneurs, academic professionals.

### 6. Sweetwater (ZIP: 33174)

(See FIU Corridor above for overlap.) Sweetwater proper (population 19,544) has a median HHI of $55,154, well below the county average, with top industries in healthcare, transportation, and retail rather than professional services [T1, ACS]. The city's 600+ businesses [T2, City of Sweetwater via FIU ETAP report] are predominantly retail and services. Professional density is low relative to other zones. It rates as a "pipeline" zone — appropriate for a later-phase chapter once surrounding zones (Kendall, Coral Gables) are established and graduates want a local option.

**Notable concentration:** Blue-collar entrepreneurs, small family businesses, first-generation immigrant business owners.

### 7. Palmetto Bay (ZIP: 33157, 33158)

Palmetto Bay is the highest-income residential-professional enclave south of Coral Gables. Median HHI is $142,447 [T1, DataUSA 2024]; average HHI reaches $205,337 [T1]. Top occupations: Management (2,630 people) and Business & Financial Operations (1,338 people) — 10.8% and 5.4% of the employed base respectively [T1, DataUSA]. The top employment sector is Professional, Scientific & Technical Services (1,816 workers) [T1]. Finance & Insurance pays a sector-average of $119,327 in the village [T1]. Homeownership at 78.2% and median property value of $879,700 signal an embedded, stable professional class, most of whom are physician-owners, attorneys, financial advisors, and executive-level corporate employees commuting to Brickell or Coral Gables [T1]. Chamber of Commerce presence through ChamberSouth anchors some business community cohesion.

**Notable concentration:** Physician-entrepreneurs, attorneys, financial advisors, C-suite executives in residential enclave mode.

---

### 8. Coconut Grove (ZIP: 33133)

Coconut Grove is Miami's tightest office micro-market. Vacancy is 2.75% — effectively full — with Class A/B blended rents at $70/SF and rising, having priced out legacy tenants toward Coral Gables [T2, Lee & Associates, Dec 2024]. Miami Today confirms Grove offices are "nearly full" with strong demand [T3, Jan 2023]. Walk scores on major office buildings exceed 90 [T2]. Despite small total inventory (one of the smallest office submarkets in the county by SF), the tenant mix is boutique financial advisors, private equity family offices, creative agencies, and boutique law firms. The neighborhood's adjacency to Brickell professionals' residential zones (waterfront condos, single-family Key) creates a concentrated weekday professional footprint. Brightline proximity and the Coconut Grove Playhouse redevelopment are sustaining demand into 2025–2026 [T3].

**Notable concentration:** Boutique PE/wealth management, family offices, attorneys, creative-class founders, real estate professionals.

---

### 9. Pinecrest (ZIP: 33156)

Pinecrest is the wealthiest residential enclave in this study. Median HHI: $192,477–$206,417 (sources vary by year, consistently top-quintile nationally) [T1, DataUSA/ACS 2023]. Population: 18,100–18,500. Top employment: Management (1,962 people), Legal (1,075 people), Sales (963 people) [T1, DataUSA]. Professional, Scientific & Technical Services is the #1 industry (1,980 workers), representing ~23% of the local workforce [T1]. Registered businesses: ~2,050 [T2, EDC South Miami-Dade]. 75% of the labor force is in white-collar professions [T2]. Median property value: $1.18M–$1.41M (2023–2024) [T1]. The density of self-employed attorneys, CPAs, and financial advisors whose practices serve affluent clients is notably high for a residential municipality. No Class A office towers — professional offices are embedded in strip-center and single-story professional parks along SW 72nd Ave and US-1 corridor.

**Notable concentration:** Attorneys, CPAs, wealth managers, physician-owners — all operating within a hyper-affluent residential catchment.

---

### 10. Aventura (ZIP: 33160, 33180)

Aventura (population ~39,885, median HHI $89,274) is a hybrid residential-commercial node anchored by the Aventura Mall corridor and Aventura Corporate Center (251,773 SF Class A campus sold in 2024 for $116.2M — $462/SF — to Adam Neumann) [T2/T3, Traded.co]. Morgan Stanley renewed 42,422 SF at the campus in a long-term commitment [T2, Blanca CRE]. Top employment sectors: Professional, Scientific & Technical Services (2,421 workers), Health Care & Social Assistance (2,224), and Retail (2,060) [T1, DataUSA]. Top occupation: Management (3,804 people) [T1]. Finance & Insurance pays $96,023 average [T1]. Brightline's Aventura station improves North–South professional connectivity. Safra National Bank HQ completion is slated for 2026 [T2]. The financial advisory community is exceptionally dense relative to population: Wells Fargo, UBS, Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley, and numerous independent RIAs all maintain dedicated Aventura offices. Justia lists 1,000+ attorneys serving the Aventura zip [T3].

**Notable concentration:** Wealth management, RIAs, private banking, real estate development, international business — heavy Israeli and South American diaspora professional community.

---

### 11. Miami Beach (ZIP: 33139, 33140, 33141)

Miami Beach (population ~82,000, median HHI $72,856) is predominantly a hospitality and residential market, but holds a significant professional services layer [T1, DataUSA]. Top industries: Accommodation & Food Services (7,230 workers) but Professional, Scientific & Technical Services ranks #2 with 6,126 workers [T1]. Top occupation: Management (7,941 people) — a higher raw count than Pinecrest or Palmetto Bay [T1]. Finance & Insurance sector averages $110,686 in wages [T1]. Startup activity is real: DoorLoop (proptech, $100M Series B 2024) is headquartered in Miami Beach [T3]. The city's median property value of $556,700 and 40.9% homeownership reflect a transient-wealthy rather than embedded-family professional demographic [T1], which creates networking community challenges. Creative agencies, tech founders, entertainment/media entrepreneurs, and real estate professionals are the dominant professional archetypes. Professional service density is high in absolute terms but diffuse; chapter retention may require hospitality-forward programming.

**Notable concentration:** Hospitality entrepreneurs, tech founders, creative-agency principals, real estate developers, entertainment/media executives.

---

### 12. Cutler Bay (ZIP: 33189, 33190)

Cutler Bay (population ~45,070, median HHI $89,244) is a growing master-planned suburban community with improving income indicators (HHI up 8.63% in 2024) [T1, DataUSA]. Top industries: Health Care & Social Assistance (3,440), Retail Trade (2,538), Construction (2,177) [T1]. Management Occupations are the top occupational category (3,225 people) despite the industry mix leaning toward health and construction [T1]. Per-capita income: $40,922 [T1, Census QuickFacts]. Professional services density is the lowest among the southern zones — the majority of high-earning residents commute to Coral Gables, Brickell, or Palmetto Bay for work rather than operating businesses locally. Without a dedicated office district, chapter convening locations would be restaurant/hotel spaces.

**Notable concentration:** Healthcare administrators, construction entrepreneurs, government employees, service-business owners.

---

### 13. Hialeah — Professional Corridor (ZIP: 33010–33018)

Hialeah (population ~221,901, second-largest city in Miami-Dade) has a median HHI of $55,594 [T1, DataUSA 2024] — the lowest in this study — and an employment base of ~115,000 workers concentrated in Health Care & Social Assistance (18,576), Construction (13,014), and Transportation & Warehousing (12,525) [T1]. Top occupations: Office & Administrative Support (13,916), Sales (11,438), Transportation (10,621) [T1]. The top-paying sector (Management of Companies) averages only $61,875 [T1] — well below comparable Coral Gables figures. Importantly, 66.5% of working residents are in professional or administrative roles per local sources [T3, AreaVibes], and 18.2% are self-employed [T3] — indicating a significant small-business-owner segment. The "professional corridor" referenced in the brief likely refers to Hialeah's NW 36th Street / Palm Avenue commercial spine, home to a concentration of insurance agencies, small CPA practices, and immigration/family law offices serving the Cuban and Venezuelan communities.

**Notable concentration:** Insurance agents, immigration attorneys, small-practice CPAs, blue-collar entrepreneurs, health clinic operators.

## Recommended Miami Launch Sequence

### Year 1 — Foundation Markets (Highest Density + Strongest Chapter Infrastructure)

**1. Brickell** (flagship)
The anchor chapter. Men who build Brickell careers are predisposed to high-performance peer accountability — hedge fund traders, attorneys, private equity principals. Monthly venue: a private dining room at a Brickell hotel (EAST, JW Marriott, Four Seasons) or a co-working rooftop (830 Brickell, WeWork). Revenue capacity: highest in portfolio. Proof-of-concept chapter.

**2. Coral Gables**
The natural second chapter. The Gables professional is a Brickell-comparable by income and career stage but with a more embedded, residential relationship to the community — University of Miami hospital physicians, multinational executives, real estate attorneys. Venue: Biltmore Hotel, Coral Gables Country Club, or The Wharf.

### Year 2 — High-Value Suburban + Specialty Markets

**3. Aventura**
Target the wealth management and private banking community clustered on Biscayne Boulevard. The Israeli-diaspora and South American investor community creates a unique high-net-worth peer group. Timing aligns with Safra Bank HQ completion and Brightline station maturation.

**4. Palmetto Bay + Pinecrest** (joint launch or co-located first chapter)
The southern residential enclave cluster. These two adjacent high-income zones share demographics (physician-owners, attorneys, CPAs with family practices) and can support one chapter with members drawn from both ZIPs. Population is too small individually for standalone chapters.

**5. Doral**
Latin corporate executive chapter. Messaging centers on import/export, Latin American market access, corporate leadership. Works well as a Spanish-language or bilingual chapter. Target: GMs and VPs at the 100 multinational HQs.

### Year 3 — Scale Markets + Emerging Zones

**6. Kendall**
Suburban independent professionals: family attorneys, CPA practice owners, insurance agents. Larger geography dilutes cohesion, but the basin is big. Year 3 timing allows time to refine the playbook before entering a longer drive-time zone.

**7. Miami Beach**
Hospitality-tech-creative chapter. Programming must be experiential (golf, boat days, rooftop dinners) given the lifestyle orientation of this demographic. High member acquisition potential; retention is the risk. Launch only after churn data from Year 1–2 cohorts is understood.

**8. Coconut Grove** (optional satellite or merge with Brickell)
Too small a submarket for a standalone chapter but could function as a "Grove meets" satellite of the Brickell chapter for members who WFH in the Grove.

**Defer:** Westchester/FIU, Sweetwater, Cutler Bay, Hialeah — revisit if the network's membership demographics indicate demand from Spanish-dominant, first-generation entrepreneur profiles.

### Five strategic findings from the Phase 1 pass

**1. Wealth Manager dominance is real and exploitable.** 563 indexed
wealth managers — 4× the next-largest category. Validates the §4
Aventura "wealth corridor" launch sequence with hard numbers.
**Recommendation:** open 2 wealth-management seats per chapter as
sub-specialties (RIA vs. Private Banker) rather than the standard 1.

**2. Attorney pools dramatically undercounted by exact-title methodology.**
Estate (7), Real Estate (8), and Family Law (13) attorney counts reflect
LinkedIn naming conventions, not actual market depth. Most practitioners
title themselves "Partner" or "Attorney" with practice area in firm bio.
True pools are 5–10× larger. **Phase-2 enhancement: cross-reference the
Florida Bar Find-a-Lawyer database** (free public records) for true
attorney counts by city + practice area.

**3. Every seat category, even the thinnest, has ≥7× oversupply per chapter.**
Brickell needs 1 estate attorney; indexed pool shows 7. Worst-case
seat-fill ratio in any category: 7:1. **No category has fill risk.**

**4. PE and M&A counts are inflated — quote with care.** PE (138) and M&A
(195) include associate-tier VPs and "Business Development" title
matches. **Use ~45–55 PE Partners and ~100–130 true M&A advisors** when
quoting decision-maker pool sizes to founders.

**5. Total addressable LinkedIn-reachable pool: 1,670 decision-makers.**
The Tribe's 10-chapter, 250-member long-term capacity represents **15%
of the LinkedIn-indexed pool** across just the 15 target seat categories.
**Headroom is structural — not a constraint.** Quotable line for the
founder pitch.

### Methodology notes

- **Geography filter:** "Miami-Fort Lauderdale Area" (LinkedIn's MSA tag,
  covers Miami-Dade + Broward + Palm Beach counties)
- **Seniority filter:** Owner/Partner + CXO + VP + Director (enforces the
  D2 75% decision-maker rule; PE row should be re-run with Owner/Partner
  + CXO only for true Partner count — counted here under standard filter
  for cross-row consistency)
- **Title filter:** Sales Nav `Current job title` (exact-title match);
  1–3 terms per row, OR'd within filter
- **Keyword bar:** intentionally empty to avoid loose full-profile matching
- **Search date:** 2026-05-03; Sales Nav Core quarterly subscription
  active through ~2026-08-03
