# D2 — Profession Composition & Category Exclusivity

> **Key Stat:** BNI chapters of 30–40 active members pass approximately 150–200+ referrals per month — roughly triple the output of a 15-member chapter. Every seat added to a fully composed chapter multiplies referral volume disproportionately because it fills contact-sphere gaps. [T2]

---

## How the Major Orgs Handle Category Overlap

### BNI — The Gold Standard of Exclusivity

BNI's foundational rule is one professional per category per chapter, with no exceptions at the chapter level. BNI states the rationale plainly: "Competition changes behavior, and BNI is intentionally designed to remove it." [T2] By holding a single seat per classification, members can "learn one person's business deeply" and "refer with clarity and confidence," knowing no competing professional will cherry-pick the same introduction. [T2]

The policy is enforced operationally: a membership committee vets incoming applicants against existing classifications, and a prospective member whose specialty overlaps a seated member's is either redirected to another chapter or asked to define a distinct sub-specialty. BNI's own materials acknowledge that sub-specialization is the practical release valve — a Miami-Dade cluster of chapters reportedly seats seventeen attorneys, each holding a distinct classification (e.g., IP litigation vs. estate planning vs. corporate M&A), so multiple lawyers can coexist across the chapter network without violating the one-per-chapter rule. [T2]

As of fiscal year ending mid-2025, BNI's 340,000+ members across 11,400+ chapters in 76 countries generated $26.4 billion in closed business from 17.4 million referrals in a single 12-month period. U.S. members averaged $62,459 in closed business annually from chapter referrals alone. [T2] The 2023 annual figure was $20.6 billion from 13.5 million referrals. [T2]

**Category conflict protocol (2024):** When a visitor's profession overlaps an existing member's seat, BNI's recommended process is: (1) identify the conflict before extending the invitation, (2) brief the seated member transparently, (3) facilitate a direct conversation between the two parties, and (4) respect the seated member's judgment. The organization has introduced the concept of "coopertition" — recognizing that two professionals in adjacent specialties may serve different client niches and can in fact generate referrals for each other rather than compete. [T2]

### ProVisors — The Non-Exclusivity Countermodel

ProVisors, founded in 1989 and now the largest U.S. network exclusively for "Trusted Advisors," operates an explicit no-exclusivity policy. Its Member Code of Conduct states: "Unlike most networking organizations, ProVisors does not promote exclusivity within a profession in a group." [T2] Two corporate attorneys, two CPAs, or two insurance professionals may join the same group provided they serve meaningfully different client niches (e.g., large-firm vs. boutique, technology clients vs. healthcare). Group Leaders hold final discretion over balance. [T2]

The rationale is relationship-density over referral monopoly: a single member may need to refer two or three qualified professionals to a client to satisfy the client's needs, or a firm's internal conflict-of-interest rules may require multiple options. [T2]

By the numbers: ProVisors grew 30% to over 7,800 members during/after COVID and posted 13% annual growth entering 2024, reaching 9,000+ members across 26 regions in early 2024 (member count now reported as **11,000+** on provisors.com as of 2026-05-03). [T1/T2] In a 2022 member survey (n ≈ 800): 69% of members provided five or more referrals to fellow members; 72% generated $5,000+ in new business from referrals; 37% generated $25,000+ in new business. [T2] The organization claims $1 billion+ in cumulative member revenue from direct referrals, though this figure is self-reported and undated. [T2] ProVisors charges approximately $150/month in dues and meets monthly rather than weekly, creating a lower time-commitment model than BNI. [T3]

**Key distinction:** ProVisors self-selects for senior, already-established advisors. Its non-solicitation rule (members cannot pitch each other; they can only respond to requests) reduces pressure but also reduces urgency. Attrition data is not publicly disclosed; the organization reports that members "remain an average of five years." [T2]

### EO (Entrepreneurs' Organization) — Non-Competing Forum Pods

EO's flagship benefit is the Forum: a confidential peer group of 6–10 entrepreneurs from non-competing industries. The non-competing rule is structural, not optional. [T2] At the broader chapter level, EO does not enforce category exclusivity — two founders in the same industry can hold chapter membership, but they are placed in separate Forum pods. EO is an identity/learning organization first; referrals are incidental, not the primary value proposition. **Minimum revenue threshold for full EO membership: $2.5M annual gross revenue** (verified 2026-05-03 from eosoflo.com); a venture-backed alternative pathway exists at $2M private / $5M public funding + 10 FTE. [T1]

### Vistage — Non-Competing Peer Advisory Groups

Vistage explicitly structures each CEO peer group to include "non-competing companies across industries." The Vistage Chair selects members to ensure "no one will be an industry rival." [T2] Group size: 12–16 members. Strict confidentiality agreements govern all group discussions. [T2] Like EO, Vistage is primarily a strategic-advisory and accountability product; referral generation is secondary. Revenue threshold: $5M+. [T2]

### YPO — Non-Competing Forum as Cultural Cornerstone

YPO requires Forum pods (its primary peer-advisory unit) to consist of 8–10 CEOs from non-competing industries. The non-competing rule exists because Forum operates under legally binding confidentiality — members share sensitive P&L data and strategic information that would create material risks if shared with a direct competitor. [T2] At the chapter level, YPO does not formally bar same-industry members from general membership, but competing individuals are separated into different Forums. Revenue/age requirements: $13M+ revenue, under 45 at joining. [T2]

### Tiger 21 — Wealth-Gated, Non-Solicitation Model

Tiger 21 groups (12–15 members) are composed of ultra-high-net-worth entrepreneurs, investors, and family office principals. Entry requires $20M+ in investable assets. The organization enforces a non-solicitation rule: members may not pitch each other. [T2] No explicit category exclusivity rule exists — multiple private equity managers or family office principals may coexist in the same group. The shared-wealth-level creates peer homogeneity without requiring occupational diversity. Competing members are handled by the group's confidentiality covenant and a culture of "genuine learning over deal-making." [T2]

### Conscious Capitalism — Values-Aligned, No Exclusivity

Local Conscious Capitalism chapters are organized around a shared business philosophy (four tenets: higher purpose, stakeholder orientation, conscious leadership, conscious culture), not around profession. No category exclusivity rules exist. Membership is open to any business leader aligned with the values. Referral generation is not a structural goal. [T2]

| **Personal Trainer / Wellness Coach** | Low | Primarily consumer-facing; limited access to the decision-maker financial events that drive cross-referrals in a professional-services group. [T3] | Nutritionist, yoga studio, spa — not cross-pollinating with core B2B professions |

## Categories That Destabilize

The following category types create friction rather than cohesion in a high-end professional networking group. They should be excluded or capped.

1. **Two direct competitors in the same sub-specialty.** Even under a "coopertition" framing, placing two competing estate attorneys or two competing wealth managers in the same room reliably produces passive non-referral behavior. Members default to not recommending someone they perceive as a competitor, reducing their referral contribution to zero in that category. BNI documented this behavioral dynamic as the core reason for its one-per-category rule. [T2]

2. **High-volume, low-relationship transaction professionals.** Categories like Groupon-style promotional products, franchise operators, or high-turnover retail businesses generate few referral relationships because their client interactions are transactional and non-recurring. They collect referrals but rarely give them into the professional-services core.

3. **Solo practitioners with narrow geographic or demographic reach.** A single-location chiropractor, solo immigration attorney, or independent bookkeeper serves a highly specific client segment that may not overlap with the CRE broker or M&A attorney across the table. The referral delta — the gap between referrals given and received — grows wide and breeds resentment. [T3]

4. **Over-weighted financial services without legal counterbalance.** Placing three wealth managers, two insurance brokers, and one CPA in a 30-person chapter without corresponding legal representation creates category bloat in one sphere and referral orphans in others. These professionals will all compete passively for the same CPA's attention. [T2]

5. **Categories with regulatory non-solicitation constraints.** Some licensed professions (certain securities brokers under FINRA rules, for instance) face compliance restrictions on referral solicitation that make active participation in a referral-first group structurally difficult. Their membership appears low-engagement. [T2]

6. **Consumer-facing "nice to have" services.** Travel agencies, florists, and restaurant owners generate high-frequency referrals in consumer groups but contribute little to the B2B and professional-services referral flows that define a high-end men's networking tribe. They dilute the room's business gravity.

## Recommended Roster for a 30-Member South Florida Men's Tribe Chapter

The following roster is calibrated for the Miami/Broward market, with its concentration of private wealth, family offices, commercial real estate development, and inbound "Wall Street South" financial firms. [T2] The chapter should target professionals primarily serving businesses or high-net-worth individuals. The target deal size per referral should average $10,000+.

**Guiding principles:**
- One member per sub-specialty (not just per broad category)
- Categories concentrated in the legal-financial-property triangle, which historically generates the highest reciprocal referral flow [T2]
- No more than 20% of seats (6 of 30) from any single BNI "contact sphere"
- Minimum 75% of members must be owners, partners, or C-suite principals — decision-makers only

**Proposed 30-Seat Category Roster:**

| # | Category | Sub-specialty Guidance | Max Per Chapter |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | CPA / Accounting | Business/transactional focus; no solo tax preparers | 1 |
| 2 | Estate Planning Attorney | Trusts, wills, wealth transfer | 1 |
| 3 | Business / M&A Attorney | Corporate transactions, entity formation | 1 |
| 4 | Real Estate Attorney (Commercial) | Commercial leasing, title disputes | 1 |
| 5 | Wealth Manager / RIA | AUM-based, $2M+ client minimum preferred | 1 |
| 6 | Life Insurance / Estate Planning Insurance | Buy-sell, key-man, premium finance | 1 |
| 7 | Commercial P&C Insurance | Commercial lines; not personal auto-only | 1 |
| 8 | Commercial Real Estate Broker | Investment sales or tenant rep; not purely residential | 1 |
| 9 | Residential Real Estate Broker (Luxury) | $1M+ market; feeds mortgage, insurance, legal | 1 |
| 10 | Commercial Lender / Private Banker | SBA, CRE debt, or HNW private banking | 1 |
| 11 | Mortgage Broker (High-End) | Jumbo/non-QM; serves same clients as luxury RE | 1 |
| 12 | M&A / Business Broker | Lower-middle-market deals; feeds CPA and attorney | 1 |
| 13 | Family Law Attorney | Divorce generates wealth-transfer and real estate needs | 1 |
| 14 | Immigration Attorney (Business) | EB-5, investor visas; high HNW overlap in South FL | 1 |
| 15 | Private Equity / Family Office Principal | Deal flow, co-investment; strategic gravity in room | 1 |
| 16 | CFO Services / Fractional CFO | Serves growth companies; refers heavily to CPA and attorneys | 1 |
| 17 | Business Coach / EOS Implementer | Must have a verifiable book of business with owner-clients | 1 |
| 18 | Executive Recruiter (C-Suite) | Touches businesses at inflection points that generate legal and financial referrals | 1 |
| 19 | Commercial Contractor / Developer | Ground-up or renovation; feeds CRE, lending, insurance | 1 |
| 20 | Commercial Insurance (Benefits / HR) | Group health/benefits for businesses with 10+ employees | 1 |
| 21 | Tax Strategist / IRS Resolution | Complements CPA without overlapping general accounting | 1 |
| 22 | PR / Communications (Corporate) | Thought-leadership PR for executives; not consumer PR | 1 |
| 23 | Technology / Cybersecurity (B2B) | MSP or cybersecurity serving professional service firms | 1 |
| 24 | Financial Advisor (Independent/Fiduciary) | Distinct from seat #5 by fee structure or client minimum | 1 |
| 25 | Title Company / Title Attorney | Closes every real estate transaction in the chapter | 1 |
| 26 | Business Banker (Community/Regional) | Operational checking, treasury; distinct from private banker | 1 |
| 27 | Healthcare Provider (Concierge / Executive) | Direct primary care serving HNW executives; distinct from mass-market medicine | 1 |
| 28 | Luxury Automotive / Asset Finance | Serves HNW lifestyle transactions; generates goodwill | 1 |
| 29 | Aviation / Charter / Yacht Broker | South Florida-specific luxury asset category; client crossover with CRE and wealth | 1 |
| 30 | Board Member / Open Strategic Seat | Reserved for highest-value gap identified by leadership in year one | 1 |

**Hard rule:** Seats 1–16 constitute the non-negotiable "core" — the professional-services triangle. Seats 17–30 are "amplifier" categories that add breadth and South Florida market-specificity. The chapter should reach 20 core seats before filling amplifier positions.
