# D5: South Florida Competitive Landscape — Men's Business Networking, Executive Peer, and Mastermind Organizations

> **Key Stat Callout:** South Florida (Miami-Dade + Broward + Palm Beach) hosts more than **80 BNI chapters**, at least **23 ProVisors home groups**, multiple Vistage groups led by ~15+ chairs, and active YPO, EO, and Tiger 21 presences — yet no organization in the region combines executive-caliber peer accountability with intentional men's work, breathwork-informed culture, and spiritual development. That gap is the tribe's primary market opening.

## Per-Org Profiles

### BNI (Business Network International)

BNI operates three distinct franchise territories in South Florida: BNI Miami-Dade (30+ chapters; Executive Director: Sandy Donovan), BNI Broward / Gold Coast (30+ chapters), and BNI South Florida Palm Beach/Southeast Florida (Sandy Donovan also covers this region) [T1]. Chapters average 20–40 members and meet weekly, typically for 90 minutes [T1]. The model is strictly one-member-per-profession, structured around a "Givers Gain" referral philosophy. Annual new member dues for Miami-Dade run $1,398 for one year or $4,146 for five years; renewals drop to $1,099/yr [T1]. BNI's primary asset is its referral volume and geographic saturation. Its primary liability is the wide variance in member quality — chapters ranging from elite professionals to commodity service providers frequently coexist within the same meeting room — and its transactional, commodity-networking identity, which lacks prestige signaling for high-earning executives. The time commitment (weekly, in-person) is a recurring criticism from senior professionals [T3].

### ProVisors

ProVisors positions itself as the largest U.S. networking organization for "trusted advisors" — attorneys, CPAs, investment bankers, financial advisors, insurance professionals, and consultants [T1]. With over 11,000 members nationally (13% annual growth entering 2024; 10,000+ by Q4 2024) [T2], the South Florida region runs 23 home groups plus four affinity groups (General South Florida, Estate & Succession Planning, Real Estate, Women's) [T1]. Meetings are structured 2-hour morning sessions; members must be in senior/partner-level roles. Estimated dues are approximately $150/month (~$1,800/yr) based on member-reported figures [T3]. ProVisors is co-ed and profession-gated rather than industry-gated, creating rich cross-disciplinary introductions. However, it does not offer peer accountability, personal development programming, or any identity beyond professional referral exchange. It functions as a structured business-development channel, not a peer community.

### EO South Florida (Entrepreneurs' Organization)

EO South Florida spans Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach, and by 2019 reporting numbered 200+ members running $2B+ in collective annual revenue [T2]. Nationally and globally, EO requires a founder or CEO role with minimum $2.5M annual company revenue [T1]. Annual global dues are $2,630 plus a $3,500 one-time initiation fee; chapter-level dues are additional [T1]. The cornerstone of EO membership is the Forum — a confidential monthly gathering of 6–10 members operating under a trained peer-facilitator model, using the Gestalt Protocol to share experience rather than give advice [T1]. EO is widely regarded as the gold standard of peer-accountability groups for growth-stage entrepreneurs. Its weaknesses include the revenue threshold (which disqualifies a significant portion of early-growth founders in a market like Miami), a primarily secular business-performance identity, and high combined dues-plus-time costs. The Forum model, however, is the closest analog in the market to what the tribe seeks to build — but absent any spiritual, masculine, or integrative wellness dimension.

### Vistage South Florida

Vistage operates an estimated 15+ active CEO peer groups across South Florida, led by approximately 15 identified chairs spanning Miami (7 chairs), Fort Lauderdale (5 chairs), and Palm Beach (7 chairs), with some chairs serving multiple areas [T1]. Groups consist of 12–16 CEOs from non-competing industries and meet monthly for a full day, with one-on-one coaching from the chair [T1]. CEO-group dues run approximately $1,380/month (~$16,560/yr) plus a $2,500 initiation fee [T2]. Vistage's model is chair-centric: the chair is simultaneously facilitator, coach, and salesperson. Chair quality therefore varies significantly and is the primary satisfaction driver. Vistage lacks any spiritual, wellness, or masculine identity and positions itself purely as a business-performance tool. Members skew toward established SMB and lower-middle-market CEOs; it does not carry the prestige signal of EO or YPO at the upper end of the market.

### Tiger 21 Miami

Tiger 21 operates 5+ confirmed groups in Miami (Miami 01, 03, 04, 05, 06) plus a Fort Lauderdale Family Office group, led by chairs including Avi Weintraub, Arthur Woods (co-founder, 1080 Ventures), Jeff Eisenberg, Cynthia Dalagelis, and Gil Bonwitt [T1]. Membership requires $20M in qualifying net worth; standard dues are approximately $33,000/yr with a $5,000 initiation fee; the Family Office group is $50,000/yr [T2]. Tiger 21 is explicitly post-liquidity and asset-preservation oriented — it targets members who have already exited or are managing significant wealth portfolios. Monthly meetings center on peer portfolio reviews. Tiger 21 does not serve active builders or growth-stage entrepreneurs, and has no personal development, spiritual, or masculine programming layer. Its market position is essentially orthogonal to the tribe's target member.

### YPO Miami / South Florida

South Florida has at least four active YPO chapters: YPO Miami, YPO Fort Lauderdale (100+ leaders, 10,000+ employees), YPO Miami/Fort Lauderdale Gold (for those who have "aged out" of standard YPO), and YPO Palm Beach [T1, T2]. YPO's eligibility is strict: members must hold a qualifying executive title, be under 45 years old, and lead a company with $13M+ in revenue or 50+ employees [T1]. Combined first-year cost (global + chapter dues + initiation) frequently exceeds $20,000 [T1]. YPO's primary asset is prestige and global network density. Its Forum model mirrors EO's in structure. Weaknesses include the age cap (meaningful in a market with many 40s-era second-act founders), the high financial barrier, and a primarily transactional social culture. Like EO, YPO carries no spiritual or values-aligned identity.

### Conscious Capitalism Florida

Conscious Capitalism Florida (consciouscapitalismfl.org) is the state-level chapter of Conscious Capitalism, Inc. The chapter connects purpose-driven business leaders and hosts programming around B Corp certification, DEI, and stakeholder-capitalism principles [T1]. Its apparent programming center is Tampa; Miami presence is modest and not independently chaptered. No dues are publicly listed. The organization functions more as an idea-sharing community than an accountability peer group and lacks the intimacy or exclusivity of EO/Vistage-style formats. It does, however, attract values-aligned CEOs and could represent a referral-source audience for the tribe.

## Spiritual/Wellness-Aligned Competition in S. FL

This sub-sector is nascent, fragmented, and largely disconnected from business-networking infrastructure — which represents the tribe's clearest blue-ocean space.

**ManKind Project Florida** operates a statewide network of men's Integration Groups (I-groups), with South Florida groups accessible to men who have completed the New Warrior Training Adventure (NWTA) weekend initiation [T1]. The NWTA carries an estimated $650–750 investment. MKP groups are free, peer-facilitated, and intensely emotionally focused. They are not business-networking forums and carry no wealth or achievement qualifier. The culture is deliberately egalitarian and counter-prestige. For a business-builder audience, MKP provides emotional depth but zero professional utility or peer-caliber accountability.

**Sacred Sons** is a national men's organization built around embodied practice, ceremony, and drum-led journey work [T1]. Confirmed Florida events include a Miami Gardens gathering in August 2025 and a statewide immersion in November 2025 [T1]. Sacred Sons operates through a hub-and-spoke event model with no permanent South Florida chapter. Its online membership tier ("The Circle") provides community but no local peer infrastructure. Brand cachet is growing among younger entrepreneurial men drawn to conscious masculinity, but the organization has no business-performance or accountability component.

**EVRYMAN** targets men seeking "real connection" through adventure, field trips, and ongoing men's groups [T1]. Monthly membership is $30 and includes online group calls and invitations to local events. The organization explicitly states it is still building local chapters and has no confirmed permanent Miami chapter as of mid-2025 [T1]. A founder-transition in early 2024 (Owen Marcus departing to form MELD) created organizational uncertainty [T2]. At $30/month, EVRYMAN carries a low prestige signal in executive circles and is better positioned for entry-level men's work participants than established entrepreneurs.

**Conscious Masculinity (consciousmasculinity.net)** is a Miami-origin organization founded by Cesar Rodriguez, offering a 10-week INITIATION program, ongoing Brotherhood Circle, 1-on-1 coaching, and a Wild Warrior Retreat in Costa Rica [T1]. Programming is primarily digital/online. The founder's Miami roots give it local cultural credibility, but the organization lacks a structured local in-person peer group, has no disclosed pricing, and is oriented toward personal transformation rather than professional peer accountability.

**Men of Mastery Miami** (observed on Meetup as of 2025) appears to offer recurring men's masculinity workshops but has limited web presence; the Meetup group was inaccessible during research [T3]. Likely a micro-community rather than a structured organization.

**Breathwork / journey-integration scene in S. FL:** A decentralized but active ecosystem exists. Notable nodes include BreathTribe Miami (weekly sessions; 5-day immersive training), Holotropic Breathwork retreats in Homestead (35 min from MIA; 4-session residential format facilitated by Jeffrey Warren) [T1], and Tikkun Healing Center (Loxahatchee, Palm Beach County; ayahuasca ceremony + transformational breathwork + integration circles) [T1]. This ecosystem is populated by individual practitioners rather than organized peer communities. Men's-specific breathwork circles do occur on an event basis (Eventbrite listings), but no standing men's executive breathwork circle with business-networking infrastructure was identified in S. FL.

**Heart of Men** (heartofmen.community) offers a "Breathwork and Brotherhood" program nationally but lacks a documented South Florida chapter [T1]. Their methodology combines breathwork, Warrior Dance embodiment, dyadic council, and peer circle — the closest structural analog to what the tribe envisions.

## Positioning Gap the Tribe Owns

No organization currently operating in South Florida occupies the intersection of:

1. **Executive-grade peer accountability** (Forum-level depth, EO/Vistage caliber)
2. **Intentional men's culture** (conscious masculinity, brotherhood as practiced value)
3. **Spiritual and somatic integration** (breathwork, ceremony, inner-work as normalized practice, not fringe)
4. **Business builder identity** (founders, operators, wealth-builders — not post-liquidity retirees)
5. **Prestige exclusivity** (curated membership, not open enrollment)

BNI, ProVisors, and Chamber groups occupy the referral-networking tier without any depth dimension. EO and Vistage occupy the peer-accountability tier without any spiritual or masculine identity. Tiger 21 and YPO occupy the prestige-network tier but are post-achievement or youth-capped and have no inner-work culture. ManKind Project, Sacred Sons, and EVRYMAN occupy the men's-work tier without any business-performance or peer-accountability structure.

The tribe's specific positioning gap: **a curated, invitation-only peer organization for high-earning men in S. FL that treats inner development and business performance as inseparable disciplines.** No incumbent in the region owns this frame. The closest structural comparables (EO Forum + men's retreat) exist only as separate products that members must stitch together individually. The tribe stitches them together as a single value proposition — and charges for the synthesis.

Secondary positioning opportunity: **the spiritual/somatic executive segment is underserved in Spanish-speaking Miami.** Miami's large Latin entrepreneurial community (Cuban-American, Venezuelan, Colombian business families) has strong cultural openness to brotherhood, spirituality, and family values — yet receives no culturally resonant peer organization that addresses both business and spirit. This is a defensible niche within the niche.
