# Research Binder Design

This document specifies the research deliverable Ben needs in hand by
Sunday 2026-05-10. The binder serves two purposes in sequence: first, it
is Ben's personal mastery document — he must be able to quote any stat
from memory in a founder conversation. Second, it becomes the appendix /
evidence pack inside the Engagement Proposal (Slice 1) when that draft is
written.

**Timeline note:** original deadline was Monday 2026-05-04 (12-hour
sprint). Revised to Sunday 2026-05-10 (7 days). The relaxed timeline
makes a formal implementation plan worthwhile (vs. the "just rip it"
approach a 12-hour sprint required) and opens room for primary-source
additions like a BNI chapter visit or 1–3 expert calls if Ben wants
them — see §5b below.

## 1. Purpose and audience priority

| Priority | Audience | Use |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (primary) | Ben | Study material. Read 3+ times. Internalize until any stat is recallable in conversation. |
| 2 (secondary) | Tribe founders | Leave-behind appendix attached to the Engagement Proposal. They read it alone, after the meeting, on their schedule. |

Optimization shifts that follow from the above:
- Density over polish. No filler paragraphs.
- "Key Stat" callout boxes on every page that highlight the one number
  Ben must memorize.
- "Quote-from-memory" highlight rule: bold sentences that fit on a sticky
  note and stand alone.
- Cross-references between sections so Ben can pivot in conversation
  ("that's actually related to category exclusivity — see §3").
- Citations next to every claim, with date and source tier (see §7).

## 2. Deliverables — two artifacts

### Artifact A: Evidence Binder (15–25 pages, dense)
Markdown source committed to repo, exported to PDF for reading offline.
Sections per §4 below. Designed to read like a McKinsey appendix: numbers,
charts, tables, citations, no marketing language.

### Artifact B: Stats Cheat Sheet (2 pages)
A condensed pull-out document with the **30 most important numbers** from
the binder, organized by topic. Designed to be re-read in 5 minutes
before any founder conversation. Each entry: number, one-line context,
binder section reference.

## 3. Pair-research workflow

Time pressure is real (12 hours, Monday deadline). To use Ben's hours
efficiently, work splits as follows:

| Source type | Who runs it | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Public web (academic papers, news, association websites, public reports) | AI | No login required; synthesis is the bottleneck, not access |
| Login-walled sources (LinkedIn Sales Navigator, paid Statista, BNI/ProVisors member areas, paid industry reports) | Ben drives, AI directs | AI provides exact URLs + exact info to capture; Ben pastes back; AI synthesizes |
| Local/in-person knowledge (S. FL chamber events, networking-group rumors, Ben's own footage from 4 meetings filmed) | Ben provides; AI structures | Ben already has this; AI organizes into citable form |

Working pattern: 30–45 minute focused blocks. Each block has a defined
output ("by end of this block, we know X"). Checkpoint between blocks
to re-prioritize remaining time.

## 4. Research lines — depth allocation

### Deep (must-have for proposal numbers and Ben's mastery)

**D1. Optimal chapter size**
- Dunbar's number and successor research (close-circle 15, friendship 50,
  social-recognition 150)
- Robin Dunbar's published work; modern revisions / critiques
- Social loafing thresholds (Latané, Williams, Harkins; modern replications)
- BNI's actual chapter caps and *the rationale they publish*
- ProVisors' chapter size norms
- Meeting cohesion vs. group size empirical data
- **Output:** defensible target chapter size with citations (likely 25–35)

**D2. Profession composition / category exclusivity**
- BNI's "one professional per category" rule — formal documentation,
  origin, and reported retention/referral effects
- ProVisors' looser model — what's their attrition / referral rate vs. BNI
- Which professional categories generate the most cross-referrals (CPA,
  attorney, financial advisor, insurance, realtor, mortgage, marketing,
  IT consultant, web/branding, business coach)
- Categories that destabilize (e.g., direct-competing services in same room)
- **Output:** recommended category list + max-per-category rule for tribe

**D3. Pricing benchmarks**
- BNI: dues + chapter fees + visitor fees, by region, current
- ProVisors: $1,800/yr range — current confirmation
- EO (Entrepreneurs' Organization): $2K–15K range
- Vistage: $16K+/yr
- Tiger 21: $30K+/yr
- YPO: $10K+/yr
- Local S. FL chambers: Greater Miami, Coral Gables, Doral, Aventura
- **Output:** competitive pricing chart; defensible target tier ($1,200–1,800/yr)

**D4. South Florida professional density by zone**
- Doral, Brickell, Kendall, Westchester, Sweetwater, FIU corridor,
  Palmetto Bay, Coral Gables, Coconut Grove, Pinecrest, Aventura,
  Miami Beach, Cutler Bay, Hialeah pro corridor
- For each zone: estimated count of business owners, attorneys, CPAs,
  CRE pros, financial advisors, healthcare entrepreneurs, tech founders
- Sources: LinkedIn Sales Navigator (Ben drives), US Census Bureau
  business patterns, Miami-Dade Beacon Council data, chamber rosters
- **Output:** prospect-density heatmap + ranked launch order recommendation

**D5. Competitive landscape S. FL**
- Existing networking groups in S. FL: BNI chapters (count, where, sizes
  if observable), ProVisors S. FL, EO Miami, Vistage SoFL, local
  Chambers, Conscious Capitalism Miami, mastermind groups
- For each: cost, member count if observable, positioning, weak spots
- **Output:** competitive matrix + tribe's positioning gap

**D6. Spiritual / holistic / alternative-wellness comparable orgs**
- Conscious Capitalism (chapters + ethos)
- Mankind Project (member journey, retention)
- Sacred Sons (event-based model, conversion to ongoing)
- EVRYMAN (men's circles + business overlay)
- Authentic Relating chapters (community model)
- MAPS-adjacent professional networks (psychedelic-integration aligned)
- Florida-specific psychedelic integration / breath-work / journey
  facilitator circles
- **Output:** positioning comparables + member-archetype profile that
  fits the tribe's spiritual/holistic angle

### Light (nice-to-have for Ben's side commentary)

**L1. Retention drivers** — what makes networking-group members renew vs.
churn (recent academic + practitioner data); 3–5 page summary

**L2. AI-era differentiation** — how a modern AI/content-infrastructure
tribe positions vs. legacy groups; 2–3 page argument with Ben's edge
crystalized

**L3. Profession-affinity for psychedelic-assisted therapy, breath work,
embodied practice** — which professions over-index on openness (likely:
tech founders, creatives, integrative-health practitioners, modern-
spirituality entrepreneurs); 2 pages, helps with affinity-group targeting

**L4. Affinity-group acquisition models** — rollerblading, run clubs,
ice baths, sound baths, hiking — examples from other orgs of using
affinity groups as soft-entry funnels into paid membership; 2 pages

## 7. Quality bar — citation discipline

Every numerical claim must include:
- The specific number
- A short context line
- A citation: `(Source name, Year)` inline; full URL/DOI in Appendix A
- A source tier marker: **[T1]** peer-reviewed/govt, **[T2]** industry/
  trade publication, **[T3]** practitioner blog/interview/podcast,
  **[T4]** anecdotal/Ben's observation

Tier rule: every must-have research line (D1–D6) needs at least one T1
or T2 source. T3/T4 are supplemental color, not load-bearing.

Recency rule: prefer sources from 2020 or later. Older sources allowed
for foundational research (Dunbar, social loafing classics) where the
work IS the foundation. Flag any source 2018 or earlier with `[older]`
tag so Ben knows when he's quoting bedrock vs. bleeding edge.
